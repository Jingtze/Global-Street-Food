Kimbap

Kimbap is a popular Korean street food. It consists of rice wrapped in seaweed and filled with various ingredients, such as pickled radish, carrots, cucumbers, spinach, and sometimes eggs, beef, or crab meat. The Kimbap is cut into bite-sized pieces for easy eating.

Kimbap, with its light and colorful flavor, is often enjoyed as a quick snack or light meal. Found at street stalls, markets, and convenience stores, it is one of the most accessible and popular foods in Korean street food culture.

Tteokbokki

Tteokbokki is one of Korea's most popular street snacks. It consists of chewy rice cakes cooked in a spicy sauce called gochujang. It's often served with fish cakes, a boiled egg, and chopped green onions.

Tteokbokki is ubiquitous at street stalls and markets, and is a popular snack for all ages. Its rich red color and addictive taste have made it a symbol of Korean street food culture.

Eomuk

Eomuk is a common street food in Korea, especially popular in winter. Made from minced fish mixed with starch and seasonings, it's sliced or skewered and cooked in broth.

It's often served in a hot broth, combining the delicious, chewy texture of the fish cake with the warming, flavorful broth. As an affordable and quick snack, eomuk is a popular delicacy at street stalls and night markets across South Korea.

Bungeoppang

Bungeoppang is a popular Korean street food dessert shaped like a fish. It's made from a fluffy batter baked in a mold and typically filled with red bean paste, though variations include cream, chocolate, and cheese.

With its crispy exterior and sweet filling, it's often sold like hotcakes on the streets during winter, bringing warmth and satisfaction. As one of Korea's most iconic street snacks, bungeoppang is beloved by people of all ages.

Sundae

Sundae is a common street food in South Korea. It's typically made with pig intestines, filled with vermicelli noodles, glutinous rice, vegetables, and sometimes pig's blood or other seasonings. It's then steamed or boiled and sliced before being eaten.

Sundae has a soft, chewy taste and a unique flavour, often enjoyed with chili powder, salt, or a dipping sauce. Sundae stalls are a common sight in markets and night markets across South Korea and are a beloved traditional snack.

Twigim

Twigim (Korean fried food) is one of the most common street food in Korea, serving as the Korean version of tempura. It consists of a variety of ingredients coated in batter and deep-fried, including vegetables, nori rolls, sweet potatoes, squid, and dumplings. The result is a crispy exterior while retaining the flavorful texture of the ingredients.

Twigim is often enjoyed with a dipping sauce like gochujang or soy sauce, and is often served alongside stir-fried rice cakes (tteokbokki). It is a popular combination at Korean night markets and roadside stalls.

Dak-kkochi

Dak-kkochi (chicken skewers) is a popular street food in South Korea. It features chicken pieces skewered on bamboo skewers, grilled over a teppanyaki grill or charcoal grill, and then basted with a sweet and spicy sauce or soy sauce. The result is a juicy and delicious dish.

The lightly charred taste of the chicken complements the sweet, spicy, or savory flavors of the sauce, creating a truly mouthwatering meal. As a classic street food that can be found at Korean night markets and street vendors, dak-kkochi is a popular choice for on-the-go snacks, gatherings, and late-night snacks.

Mandu

Mandu, a traditional Korean snack, is a favorite on roadside stalls and market. Made with thin dough wrapped around fillings such as pork, beef, kimchi, tofu, vermicelli noodles, or vegetables, mandu can be steamed, boiled, pan-fried, or deep-fried.

Mandu offers a variety of tastes and fillings, often served with a dipping sauce made from soy sauce, vinegar, and chili powder. A classic delicacy served at street vendors and festive tables, mandu not only provides a satisfying meal but also reflects the diversity of Korean culinary culture.
